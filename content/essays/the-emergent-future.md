---
title: "The Emergent Future of Product Development"
date: 2026-02-01T00:00:00.000Z
---

*Six months ago I started breaking down what happens when you actually build with AI every day. What I found isn't a new tool. It's a new process — and it makes Agile look like a horse and buggy.*

I want to be careful here, because "Agile is dead" is a tired take and that's not what this is. Agile was the right answer to a real problem: software development was too rigid, too waterfall, too disconnected from reality. Agile introduced feedback loops, iteration, collaboration. It made software better.

But Agile was designed for a world where the bottleneck was implementation. Where writing code was slow and expensive, so you needed to be very thoughtful about what you built and in what order. Sprints, story points, velocity — all of it is machinery for managing a scarce resource: engineering time.

What happens when that resource isn't scarce anymore?

I've been building with AI agents — primarily Claude Code — for about six months now, and the honest answer is: most of Agile's rituals stop making sense. Not because collaboration doesn't matter. It matters more than ever. But because the rhythm of the work is fundamentally different.

In a traditional sprint, you plan work, estimate it, assign it, execute it over two weeks, review it, and retro on it. The cycle time from idea to working software is measured in weeks. In an agentic workflow, the cycle time from idea to working software is measured in minutes. I describe what I want. The agent builds it. I use it. I adjust. The whole loop — the thing that sprints were designed to enable — happens in a single sitting.

This doesn't make planning irrelevant. It makes planning continuous. You're not sitting in a room on Monday morning deciding what to build this sprint. You're deciding what to build right now, based on what you just learned from the thing you built five minutes ago. The feedback loop collapsed from two weeks to five minutes, and that changes everything downstream.

Here's what I've seen emerge as the atomic ingredients of this new process.

The first ingredient is intent. You have to know what you're trying to build and why. This sounds trivial but it's actually the hardest part, because the safety net of a two-week sprint — where you could figure it out as you went — is gone. When you can build anything in minutes, the question "what should we build?" becomes urgent in a way it never was before.

The second is context. The quality of what the agent produces is directly proportional to the context you give it. Design files, architecture decisions, user research, business constraints — all of it needs to be accessible to the agent, ideally through something like MCP rather than manual copy-paste. Context engineering is becoming a discipline in its own right.

The third is craft. The agent writes code, but someone still needs to know whether the code is good. Not just "does it work" but "is this the right architecture? Will this scale? Does this feel right to the user?" That judgment doesn't come from the agent. It comes from the people directing the agent.

The fourth is speed of evaluation. When you can produce working software in minutes, the bottleneck shifts to how fast you can evaluate it. Can you look at the thing, understand what's working and what isn't, and articulate the next iteration? This is a skill. It's closer to editorial judgment than engineering skill. And the people who are best at it tend to be the ones with the broadest understanding of the problem — not necessarily the deepest technical knowledge.

The fifth is taste. I keep coming back to this word because nothing else captures it. When the cost of building drops to near zero, the differentiator is what you choose to build. The bar for "good enough" rises because everything is easy. What separates great work from mediocre work is the same thing it's always been — someone with a strong point of view about what the thing should be.

What's emerging isn't a methodology. It's more like a posture. Stay close to the material. Build to think. Evaluate fast. Trust your instincts about what's working. Bring diverse perspectives into the loop early because the cost of incorporating their input is nearly zero. Ship when it's right, not when the sprint ends.

I don't know what to call this yet. It's not Agile, because the fundamental assumptions of Agile — scarce engineering capacity, two-week iteration cycles, story-point estimation — no longer apply. It's not waterfall, obviously. It's something new. Something that takes the best of Agile's values — collaboration, iteration, user focus — and rebuilds them around a reality where building is fast and judgment is the bottleneck.

Whatever we call it, it's already here. The teams that recognize it are moving at a pace that looks impossible from the outside. The teams that don't are still running sprint planning meetings and wondering why they feel slow.
